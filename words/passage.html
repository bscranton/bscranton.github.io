The old man coughed a fit and then looked up at Karrin where she stood near the fire. His eyes were milky white with blindness but he seemed to see her just fine.

Do you hear the call of the desert?

He stayed quiet for a time while Karrin tended the fire. Watching her with his dying eyes. She tried not to notice. Her feelings toward the old man were complicated enough.

It is almost time. Tonight, maybe tomorrow. The desert will speak, you will know what to do.

He sounded more like himself, the man who had taken her in and taught her the old ways. He stood, unsteady, leaning on his staff. Karrin moved to help him but he waved her off and hobbled toward his tipi. As he closed the flap an owl called in the distance. Karrin barely noticed, lost in thought.

A storm had blown through during the night and nearly drowned the camp fire. When she woke Karrin set to work stoking the embers that had survived the deluge so she could make their morning tea. As she worked she heard a song drifting softly on the warm morning breeze. She had never heard it before and yet she knew the melody, like an old friend. Just as sure as she knew the song she knew she would need only one cup for tea this morning.

She went through the motions of their morning ritual, wondering when or if emotion would overtake her. There were times when she had hated the old man. He could be cruel and capricious. Other times he could be distant and unapproachable. But just as often he was a kind and patient teacher. And he had been her only companion for many years now. For more than half of her life. Any time she felt emotion welling up to overwhelm her the song in her head changed. It became almost a lullaby sung by a mother she barely remembered. Other times she could almost hear the old man's voice whispering amongst the notes. By the time her morning ritual was done she knew what she had to do.

She found him in his favorite grove of live oak trees on a hillock overlooking the river. It was an island of verdant green amidst and endless landscape of reds and browns. Even with death so near it was tranquil here. The old man was sitting in a stone circle that he had laid out. He had been sitting on his knees but was now slumped forward. She was shocked to see that he had eviscerated himself, an offering to the grove to accept him as their own. He still clutched an ancient stone knife in his hand. She marveled that the frail old man had been able to complete this last ritual at all.

She spent the morning gathering stones to build his cairn, glad to have a task to focus on. She took her time, collecting just the right stones. The funerary ritual was supposed to be done at dusk so the old druid's mortal remains could be at peace with the sun. She was glad to wait. To mourn the man he was while she prepared his resting place.

As dusk approached she couldn't put it off any longer. She gently lay his body face up, palms touching the ground so that even as he became one with the desert he could see the sky and feel the earth. As she lay him out and began to cover him with stones a single acorn fell and came to rest on his bare chest. The trees had accepted his offering. He would become one with this grove that he had spent a lifetime caring for.

Once the last stone was in place she whispered his name. It wasn't part of the ritual, she just wanted to make sure the grove remembered. Then she walked away under the moon, into the vast desert.
