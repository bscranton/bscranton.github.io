A long time ago a little girl named Lottie lived on a small farm by the woods with her grandmother. Lottie’s mom had disappeared into the woods one night and her dad had gone off to fight for General Grant not long after. So Lottie and her grandma kept the farm as best they could. One night when Lottie was out fetching water a man came across the field. He was dressed in a blue coat with shiny brass buttons and a blue slouch hat just like the uniform her daddy wore when he left for the war. He was kind of scary looking but Lottie screwed up her courage and spoke to the man. He said he had been in the war but he got hurt and now he was on the way home. Lottie thought he reminded her a lot of her daddy so she invited him home for dinner.

Back at the cabin Lottie told her grandma that she had brought a guest for supper.

“He looks just like Daddy did before he went off to the war.” Lottie said.

Grandma looked the man over and invited him to sit at the kitchen table and have a cup of tea while she finished getting the stew ready. The man took off his hat and sat down, grateful for a warm place to rest his feet for a little while. He sat and he sat while Grandma chopped carrots and chopped potatoes and chopped some other vegetables too. Eventually the man nodded off a bit. Grandma kept chopping and singing some little lullaby quietly to herself.

The man awoke suddenly from his nap a little while later, having had a nightmare about the battle that got him wounded and sent home. He looked around the cabin, trying to remember where he was. He saw the old woman, the little girl’s grandma sitting across from him at the table quietly drinking from a teacup. He didn’t see the little girl anywhere. The stew was starting to smell very good.

“Well Grandma, that stew smells just about done. Where’s your little granddaughter? Should I go fetch her for dinner?”, the man said.

“No, no young man. Rest your feet a while still. Little Lottie will be along soon I’m sure of it.”, said Grandma.

So the man sat a bit more, feeling very hungry and very tired. He figured the stew must be almost done and realized that he hadn’t washed up since he came inside. He set his hat down on the table and asked Grandma where the wash basin was for he sure could use a quick wash. Grandma pointed to a handsome old cast-iron wash basin back in the corner near a pair of big old beds.

So the man got up, gathered the water bucket that the young girl must have left sitting by the fire for it to warm and set about washing up. He washed his hands and he washed his face. He used a little extra water to wash his hair a bit to be a little more presentable once supper got started.

As he was turning to walk back to the table he noticed an odd shape under the blankets on the smaller bed. About the size of a little girl only somehow smaller. But he also saw some stains. On the side of the mattress, on the duvet and a little on the wall. He turned to look a little closer, turned away from the mirror over the wash basin.

Those stains looked mighty familiar. They looked like the stains on the bandages from the field hospital where the doctors had saved his life on the battlefield at Petersburg. The man couldn’t explain why if you asked him, but he felt a powerful urge to pull back those blankets.

And so he did. And he saw the skeleton of a child. A little girl by the look of the long auburn locks still attached to the skull and the pretty little dress she still wore. But that dress looked awful familiar. Just like the dress little Lottie was wearing when she invited him home for supper.

The thought occurred to him to run away from this place but he was frozen in confusion and not some small amount of fear. Those terrible stains covered the bed under the blankets too. And the skeleton. And the dress. The man had just started to turn and run when he felt a powerful hand yank his hair back. A powerful foot pressed against the inside of his knee. He was forced to kneel and his head was held painfully back.

Then a horrible new wave of pain seared across his neck. New red stains started to cover the bed. Started to cover the wall and the blankets and the dress and the little skeleton too. The whole world started to go a little dark and a little sidewise and the last thing the man ever heard was Grandma saying in her sweet old-lady voice, “Good job Lottie, this one’s bones will make excellent stew.”
